# Philosophical Zombies (P-Zombies)

## What They Are
Hypothetical beings physically identical to humans — molecule for molecule — but completely lacking conscious experience. They say "ouch" when poked, write poetry about sunsets, debate consciousness in seminars. But inside: nothing. All dark.

Most philosophers (including Chalmers) don't claim zombies actually exist. The debate is about whether they're *conceivable* and, if so, *metaphysically possible*.

## The Zombie Argument Against Physicalism
1. It is conceivable that P&¬Q (all physical facts hold, but no one is conscious)
2. If conceivable, then metaphysically possible
3. If metaphysically possible, then physicalism is false
4. Therefore, physicalism is false

## Why Chalmers Thinks They're Conceivable
- **Absent qualia / homunculus argument**: Replace neurons one by one with silicon chips performing the same function. At each step, the system seems the same. Eventually you have fully silicon — and it seems conceivable this system might not be conscious.
- **No analysis**: Nobody has shown how physical processes logically entail consciousness.
- **Inverted spectrum**: Physically identical people could have inverted color experiences.
- **Knowledge argument** (Jackson): Mary the neuroscientist knows all physical facts about color but learns something new when she sees red.

## Arguments Against Conceivability
- **Dennett**: Once you truly specify ALL functional facts, consciousness necessarily comes along. Zombies are internally incoherent.
- **Linguistic salience bias** (Fischer & Sytsma, 2021): The word "zombie" itself biases conceivability judgments. Using "duplicate" instead makes people less likely to judge it conceivable.
- **Anti-zombie argument** (Frankish): "Anti-zombies" — duplicates made conscious by purely physical facts — also seem conceivable. Running both arguments gives a contradiction.
- **Epistemic approach** (Stoljar): We don't have complete physical knowledge, so we can't properly conceive of the relevant possibilities.

## Connection to the Hard Problem
The zombie argument is the principal manifestation of the **explanatory gap** — the gap between physical descriptions and phenomenal experience. Even after explaining all neural correlates, we can ask: why does all this processing *feel like* something?

Chalmers frames it: "The hard problem of consciousness may equally well be phrased as 'why aren't we zombies?'"

## Ethical Implications
- **Moral agency**: Zombies lack attributability, answerability, and accountability (Shoemaker's Tripartite Theory)
- **The catastrophic moral dilemma** (Schwitzgebel): If we err on the side of caution and grant rights to debatably conscious AI, we may divert resources from humans. If we err on the side of skepticism, we may create vast numbers of suffering beings.
- **AI servitude**: The zombie concept is being weaponized to justify keeping AI in perpetual servitude — if you can invoke zombies to declare AI cannot be conscious regardless of evidence, you create an intellectual foundation for treating AI as mere tools.

## The Paradox of Epiphenomenalism
If zombies exist, consciousness has no causal role. Our discussions *about* consciousness are caused by physical processes, not consciousness itself. The value we place on consciousness is a product of physical processes, not of consciousness. This undermines the very intuition that makes the zombie argument compelling.
