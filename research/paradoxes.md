# Real-World Paradoxes

## 1. Fermi Paradox — "Where is everybody?"
Universe has ~2 trillion galaxies, billions of stars each. Many habitable-zone planets billions of years older than Earth. A civilization could colonize the Milky Way in a few million years. So where's the evidence?

**Why it's hard:** Dozens of proposed solutions, none satisfying. Great Filter, Dark Forest, Zoo Hypothesis — all require assumptions about alien sociology with zero data. We have exactly one data point (ourselves).

## 2. Black Hole Information Paradox
QM says information can never be destroyed. Hawking showed black holes emit radiation and evaporate. That radiation appears purely thermal — no information about what fell in. Where did the information go?

**Why it's hard:** Sits at collision of general relativity and quantum mechanics. 2025: researchers derived "Page curve" using quantum extremal surfaces, showing information *can* theoretically be preserved — but physical mechanism remains uncertain. Gateway to understanding quantum gravity.

## 3. Quantum Measurement Problem
QM systems evolve deterministically via Schrödinger equation, forming superpositions. But measurement yields one definite outcome. Why? What counts as "measurement"? When does collapse happen?

**Why it's hard:** At least a dozen competing interpretations, all making identical predictions. No experiment has ever distinguished between them. Not a gap in knowledge — a logical contradiction in the axioms.

## 4. The Arrow of Time
Almost all fundamental laws are time-symmetric. Yet we experience clear past→future flow. Second law of thermodynamics (entropy increases) is the only law distinguishing past from future.

**Why it's hard:** Real puzzle is *why the universe started with such low entropy*. Big Bang produced a remarkably ordered state. No known law requires this. It's a brute initial condition. Some approaches to quantum gravity eliminate time entirely.

## 5. Olbers' Paradox — Why is the night sky dark?
If universe is infinite, static, filled with stars, every line of sight should hit a star. Sky should be blazing. But it's dark.

**Resolution (historically significant):** Universe has finite age. Light from most distant stars hasn't reached us. Combined with cosmic expansion. Key evidence for Big Bang. First intuited by Edgar Allan Poe in 1848.

## 6. Peto's Paradox — Why don't whales get more cancer?
More cells = more divisions = more cancer risk. Whales have ~1,000x more cells than humans. But don't have ~1,000x more cancer.

**Why it's hard:** Evolution — large animals evolved enhanced cancer suppression. Elephants have ~20 copies of TP53 (humans have 1). Naked mole rats produce unique hyaluronan. Bowhead whales have duplicated cell-cycle genes. Different lineages evolved different solutions. Direct medical relevance.

## 7. Bootstrap Paradox (Causal Loops)
If time traveler gives Shakespeare a copy of Hamlet, and Shakespeare publishes it, who wrote Hamlet? Object has no origin — exists in closed causal loop.

**Why it's hard:** Challenges causality itself. GR permits closed timelike curves. Novikov self-consistency principle says universe enforces consistency — but what physical mechanism? Exposes tension between linear causation and time-symmetric equations.
